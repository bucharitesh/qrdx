QR codes feel like magic: aim a camera, tap a link, done. Under the hood they are a carefully standardized **binary barcode** — a tiny grid of black and white modules that encodes bits, protects them with math, and arranges them so decoders can read them from almost any angle.

This post unpacks how that works, without hand-waving. If you build or design with QR codes, this is the mental model worth having.

## QR codes vs. traditional barcodes

A **1D barcode** (like UPC) encodes data in stripes of varying width along a single line. It is dense in one dimension and fragile in the other: smudges or perspective can wipe out a whole scan line.

A **QR code** (Quick Response) is **2D**: data lives in a **square matrix of modules** (usually black on white). You get more capacity in the same printed area, and the format is built for **omnidirectional** reading — the decoder figures out orientation from the pattern itself.

That orientation trick is why you see the same “bullseye” squares in every QR code: they are not decoration; they are **alignment machinery**.

## The physical grid

A QR symbol is a square of **modules** (think pixels, but printed). The smallest standard version is **Version 1**: **21×21** modules. Each step up adds **4 modules per side**:

- Version 1 → 21×21  
- Version 2 → 25×25  
- Version 40 → 177×177 (the largest defined in the common standard)

Bigger versions exist to carry **more data** and **more error correction redundancy**, not because the logo got bigger.

### Finder patterns

Three **finder patterns** sit in the **top-left**, **top-right**, and **bottom-left** corners. Each is a 7×7 module square: a 3×3 black core, a one-module white ring, and a 7×7 black outer border.

Scanners hunt for these shapes first. Because there are **three** of them, software can infer **scale**, **rotation**, and **perspective** — that is how your phone still works when the sticker is skewed on a curved cup.

### Timing patterns

**Timing patterns** are alternating black/white runs between finder patterns. They help the decoder **count modules** and stay synchronized while walking the grid — like a ruler printed into the code.

### Alignment patterns (larger versions)

From **Version 2** upward, smaller **alignment patterns** appear in the interior. They refine position when the surface is curved or the image is distorted — think bottle labels and flexible packaging.

### Quiet zone

The standard expects a **margin of light modules** around the symbol (the **quiet zone**). Without it, adjacent graphics can merge visually with the code and confuse the edge detector. In practice: **always leave whitespace** around a QR; your design tool should enforce it.

## What gets encoded: bit streams and segments

At the lowest level, a QR code stores a **bit stream** made of:

1. **Mode indicators** — how the next chunk should be interpreted  
2. **Character count** — how long that chunk is  
3. **Payload** — the actual data  
4. **Terminator / padding** — fill out to a byte boundary  
5. **Error correction codewords** — Reed–Solomon redundancy  

### Data modes

The encoder chooses a **mode** that fits the payload efficiently:

| Mode | Typical use | Idea |
|------|-------------|------|
| **Numeric** | Digits only | Packs triples of digits into 10 bits |
| **Alphanumeric** | `0–9`, `A–Z`, space, `$%*+-./:` | 5.5 bits per character (pairs) |
| **Byte** | Arbitrary bytes (URLs, UTF-8) | 8 bits per byte |
| **Kanji** | Shift JIS kanji | Compact for Japanese text |

A single QR can mix **multiple segments** with different modes in one symbol — for example, an optimized numeric chunk followed by a byte-mode URL.

### Capacity vs. error correction

You cannot fill a QR to the theoretical “maximum characters” in isolation. **Error correction** eats a large fraction of the codewords. The same version 5 symbol holds **more raw data** at **Level L** (low recovery) than at **Level H** (high recovery).

Rough intuition:

- **Level L** — ~7% recovery — largest payload, smallest robustness  
- **Level M** — ~15%  
- **Level Q** — ~25%  
- **Level H** — ~30% — smallest payload, survives abuse (partial occlusion, dirt)

Marketing QRs on outdoor signage often use **M or Q**. Tiny codes on screens can sometimes use **L** if the environment is clean.

## Error correction: why a torn code still scans

QR codes use **Reed–Solomon** codes over GF(256). In plain language: extra **parity-like** bytes are computed from your message so the decoder can **reconstruct missing or wrong modules** up to a limit.

That is why a corner can be covered by a finger or a logo (if you respect the standard’s limits) and the payload still recovers — you are not “guessing the URL,” the math is **reconstructing the bit stream**.

This is also why **central logos** are safe only when you treat them as **occlusion**: you must **lower effective data** or **raise version / EC level** so redundancy covers the blocked modules.

## Mask patterns — making the code easier to read

After data and error bytes are placed on the grid according to placement rules, the encoder **XORs** the data area with one of **eight mask patterns**. The goal is to avoid **large uniform regions** and **confusing patterns** that look like finder components.

The standard defines a **penalty score** for each mask; the encoder picks the mask that **minimizes** that score. Same URL, same EC level — the **black/white arrangement can change** because a different mask won.

You do not choose masks by hand in normal tools; the generator does it for you.

## What happens when you scan

A modern pipeline looks roughly like:

1. **Image capture** — camera frame, possibly HDR  
2. **Find finder patterns** — locate the three squares  
3. **Homography / perspective warp** — flatten the grid  
4. **Sample modules** — decide black vs. white per cell (thresholding adapts to lighting)  
5. **Read format info** — mask, EC level, and data mask applied  
6. **Extract & correct codewords** — Reed–Solomon recovery  
7. **Parse segments** — modes and payload  
8. **Hand off** — URL, Wi‑Fi config, vCard, etc.

Different apps implement steps with different heuristics, which is why **contrast**, **quiet zone**, and **module size** still matter in the real world even though the math is forgiving.

## Design implications (the short version)

None of this replaces taste — but it bounds what is safe:

- **Quiet zone** is part of the spec, not optional trim.  
- **Contrast** along the red channel matters for many cameras (avoid light gray on white).  
- **Logos** trade **payload capacity** for **style**; generators should validate **readability**, not just aesthetics.  

Tools like **QRdx** exist to keep that balance: full customization **with** a live preview so you see when you have pushed contrast, patterns, or logos too far.

## Further reading

The widely implemented specification is **ISO/IEC 18004** (QR Code model 2). Open-source encoders and decoders (ZXing, qrcode, etc.) implement the same rules — if you read one library’s “QR format” docs, you are reading a friendly version of the same standard.
